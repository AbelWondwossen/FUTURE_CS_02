## 🛠️ Analysis Methodology
To measure the "maliciousness" of each attempt, I used a dual-verification workflow:

*   **MXToolbox (Header Analyzer):** Used to parse the `.eml` headers. I focused on the `Authentication-Results` to confirm **SPF**, **DKIM**, and **DMARC** failures.
*   **VirusTotal (IP Reputation):** The source IP from the `Received` header was scanned against 70+ antivirus engines to check for previous blacklisting or phishing reports.

---

## Case Study 1: CEO Fraud (BEC)
**Scenario:** An urgent wire transfer request impersonating the CEO.
*   **Vector:** Business Email Compromise (BEC).
*   **MXToolbox Result:** `SPF: FAIL`. The IP `185.142.53.102` is not permitted to send for the company domain.
*   **VirusTotal Result:** IP flagged by **Fortinet** and **Sophos** as a known phishing source.
*   **File:** `example1_ceo.eml`

## Case Study 2: Shipping Scam (UPS)
**Scenario:** Fake delivery failure notice requesting an address update.
*   **Vector:** Credential Harvesting.
*   **MXToolbox Result:** `DMARC: FAIL`. Reply-To points to a look-alike domain (`ups-delivery-service.com`).
*   **VirusTotal Result:** IP `103.45.67.89` flagged for hosting malicious redirection scripts.
*   **File:** `example2_ups.eml`

## Case Study 3: Account Suspension (Netflix)
**Scenario:** Threat of permanent account closure due to "unusual activity."
*   **Vector:** Financial Information Theft.
*   **MXToolbox Result:** `DKIM: none`. No digital signature present, indicating the email was generated by a generic mail-server script.
*   **VirusTotal Result:** IP `185.130.5.253` categorized as **Malicious** with multiple community reports of Netflix phishing.
*   **File:** `example3_netflix.eml`
